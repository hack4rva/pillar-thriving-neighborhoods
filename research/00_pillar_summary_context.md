## 3. Key Metrics

### 3.1 National LIHTC Landscape

The HUD LIHTC database contains information on 54,102 projects and 3.7 million housing units placed in service between 1987 and 2023 [1]. Data for properties placed in service in 2024 will be collected in the fall of 2025 and added to this database in the spring of 2026 [1]. 

**Implication:** The LIHTC program remains the core financing engine for affordable housing in the U.S.; any local incentive must align with LIHTC eligibility criteria to tap this capital stream. 

### 3.2 Los Angeles City Inventory

The Los Angeles GeoHub tracks affordable housing developments across the city, providing critical location data for 2,381 projects [2]. Approximately 9,300 units are documented as affordable, with a significant portion located within transit-oriented development overlays.

**Implication:** LA's zoning and transit-oriented policies are already yielding a modest clustering of affordable units near transit, suggesting that expanding TOD incentives could accelerate production. 

### 3.3 Washington DC Production

The District of Columbia's affordable housing dataset includes 922 projects (production and preservation, rental and for-sale) subsidized by DMPED, DHCD, DCHFA, or DCHA, and those produced as a result of Planned Unit Development (PUD) proffers or Inclusionary Zoning (IZ) requirements [3]. Furthermore, municipal measures such as offering city-owned sites for affordable housing lease to non-profits have proven effective in other jurisdictions like Metro Vancouver [5].

**Implication:** Direct city-land leverage is a proven tool; replicating a city-owned-site lease program could be a high-impact lever for our jurisdiction. 

### 3.4 Seattle Design-Review Streamlining

Seattle's Ordinance 126854 (CB 120581) relates to Design Review for affordable housing, adopting temporary regulations to exempt housing projects that meet Mandatory Housing Affordability requirements using on-site performance units from Design Review [4]. This ordinance was passed on July 5, 2023, and signed by the Mayor on July 14, 2023 [4].

**Implication:** Reducing review time can lower soft-costs and increase developer appetite for affordable-unit delivery. 

## 4. Risk & Opportunity Matrix

| Dimension | Opportunity | Risk / Mitigation |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Financing** | Leverage the LIHTC database to target under-served census tracts; apply for upcoming federal LIHTC allocations [1]. | LIHTC credit caps may limit available funding; mitigate by pairing with State-Level Tax Credit programs. |
| **Land Supply** | Use city-owned site leases to acquire low-cost parcels for non-profits [5]. | Acquisition costs can surge in hot markets; conduct pre-emptive zoning to earmark "Affordable-Housing Zones." |
| **Regulatory** | Adopt Seattle-style design-review exemptions to cut permit lead-times for projects meeting affordability mandates [4]. | Potential community push-back on perceived "light-touch" reviews; include public-engagement checkpoints to preserve transparency. |
| **Data-Driven Targeting** | GeoHub and DC Open Data enable GIS-based site selection, focusing on transit corridors & high-need neighborhoods [2] [3]. | Data quality gaps may distort analysis; implement a data-validation protocol before policy roll-out. |

*Takeaway:* While financing and land acquisition present inherent risks, proactive regulatory streamlining and data-driven targeting offer robust mitigation pathways.

## 5. Recommendations

1. **Integrate LIHTC Screening:** Build a semi-annual query pipeline using HUD's interactive tool (https://www.huduser.gov/lihtc/) to identify projects eligible for additional local subsidies [1]. 
2. **Pilot a City-Owned-Site Lease Program:** Start with 5 pilot parcels in the city's Transit-Oriented Development zones; offer 20-year below-market leases with a strict affordable-unit set-aside, mirroring successful municipal measures [5]. 
3. **Enact a Design-Review Exemption Ordinance:** Model after Seattle's CB 120581, limiting the exemption to projects that meet Mandatory Affordability via the performance option [4]. Attach a 5-year sunset clause for periodic review. 
4. **Deploy a GIS Dashboard:** Combine local GeoHub data, Open Data portals, and the LIHTC dataset into a single interactive map for internal decision-makers, refreshed quarterly [2] [3]. 
5. **Establish a Data-Quality Task Force:** Assign a cross-departmental team to audit missing fields in local housing datasets, prioritize missing unit-type and financing-source information, and document remediation steps.
