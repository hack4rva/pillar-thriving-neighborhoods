# Richmond’s Neighborhoods Now: Where Growth Meets Displacement Risk

## Executive Summary
Richmond is experiencing a profound neighborhood transformation driven by concentrated private investment, rising housing costs, and shifting zoning frameworks. Development is heavily concentrated in a handful of urban nodes—specifically Scott’s Addition, Manchester, Shockoe Bottom, and the Jackson/Monroe Ward corridors [1] [2]. While multifamily supply is rising and rent growth is cooling (stabilizing at 95.2% occupancy with 2.2% year-over-year rent growth) [3], the baseline affordability has fundamentally shifted. Rents in the city have surged 23.7% since the pandemic, and 30.37% across the broader metro area [4]. 

This growth is colliding directly with displacement risks. Eviction filings have rebounded to 95% of pre-pandemic levels citywide, and have actively surpassed pre-pandemic baselines in the exact ZIP codes seeing the most intense reinvestment (23219, 23220, 23224, 23225, and 23230) [5]. While the City is scaling its response—committing ARPA funds to deliver roughly 2,135 affordable units by 2026 and awarding $9 million from the Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF) [6] [7] —near-term delivery lags, with zero new renter-occupied units completed through federal programs in the most recent program year [6]. The upcoming "Code Refresh" zoning overhaul presents a critical leverage point to mandate inclusive growth before land values escalate further [8].

## Purpose and Decision Frame
The core challenge for the Thriving Neighborhoods pillar is determining where to intervene for maximum neighborhood equity. Concentrated growth is colliding with severe affordability stress, meaning decisions must pair development approvals with anti-displacement measures and targeted affordability where pressure is highest. By focusing public resources, zoning incentives, and tenant protections on the specific nodes absorbing the most capital, Richmond can harness private momentum to fund public benefits rather than accelerate displacement.

## What’s Changing and Where
Five core nodes are absorbing the vast majority of new capital. The pending zoning updates will determine whether this growth remains exclusive or becomes inclusive.

### Scott’s Addition: The Epicenter of Urban Growth
Scott's Addition remains the primary engine of Richmond's multifamily boom, transitioning from an industrial district to a high-density residential and entertainment hub [1] [3]. The neighborhood recently saw Hoffman & Associates and DeBartolo Development secure $144 million in financing for a 366-unit mixed-use project at 3200 W Moore St, which will include 18,000 square feet of retail space [3]. Metro-wide, multifamily supply growth remains strong with 6,627 apartments underway as of August 2025 [3]. However, the heavy concentration of luxury product requires the City to negotiate on-site affordability and workforce units in remaining large parcels to sustain absorption and ensure mixed-income access.

### Manchester: Densifying the South Riverfront
Manchester continues to be the most active residential and commercial development hub south of the James River [1]. Developers are pursuing high-density projects with ground-floor retail, creating true mixed-use corridors along Hull Street and Semmes Avenue [1]. The combination of waterfront views and lower land costs has attracted national investors [1]. However, this rapid densification is occurring in ZIP code 23224, which is simultaneously experiencing a surge in eviction filings [5].

### Jackson Ward and Monroe Ward: Infill and Cultural Heritage
Monroe Ward and Jackson Ward bridge the downtown core and the VCU campus, offering some of the highest walkability scores in the city [1]. Developers are focusing on infill multifamily and adaptive reuse of historic mid-rises to capture student, professional, and medical employee demand [1]. Because this area overlaps with ZIP codes 23219 and 23220—both of which have seen eviction filings surpass pre-pandemic levels [5] —cultural preservation overlays and inclusionary targets are urgently needed.

### Shockoe Bottom and Church Hill: Transit-Oriented Momentum
Shockoe Bottom is emerging as a premier transit-oriented development (TOD) node. The City's recent RFP for 212 N. 18th Street highlights the area's exceptional transit access, sitting just 0.1 miles from Main Street Station and 0.2 miles from the GRTC Pulse BRT [2]. Market momentum is clearly signaled by the newly completed 212-unit mixed-use development known as "The Bakery" immediately across the street [2]. The City has a prime opportunity to hardwire equity via RFP requirements for mixed-income targets and ground-floor activation.

### Carver, Brookland Park, and the Diamond District
Additional growth nodes such as Carver, Brookland Park, and the Diamond District are seeing increased investment interest [2]. This early-stage pressure offers the City a chance to set equitable rules and secure land control before speculative land values run up.

## Housing Affordability and Stability
Rents have risen dramatically since 2020, and while growth is currently moderating, the new baseline has left a massive portion of the city cost-burdened.

### Rents Rose Fast, Now Moderating
Since the pandemic, apartment rental prices in the City of Richmond have increased by 23.70%, while the broader metro area has seen a 30.37% increase [4]. As of February 2026, the overall average rent in Richmond stands at $1,446 [4]. However, rent growth has recently slowed significantly; year-over-year growth is at 1.46%, and trailing three-month growth softened to 0.2% [3] [4]. This cooling provides a window to expand supply while deploying targeted subsidies in the ZIP codes that experienced the largest run-ups.

### Cost Burden Remains High with Racial Disparities
Housing cost burden is structurally embedded in Richmond's housing market. Approximately 36,630 households (42% of the city) are housing cost-burdened [9]. This burden disproportionately impacts minority residents: 50% of Black households (19,620 households) are cost-burdened [9]. Furthermore, 57% of Richmond households are renters, and 53% of those renters have unaffordable housing costs (paying more than 30% of their income) [10]. Addressing this requires scaling down payment assistance, supporting Community Land Trusts, and requiring 60–80% AMI workforce units in new approvals.

### Evictions Near or Beyond Pre-Pandemic Levels in Hot ZIPs
Eviction pressure is highest in the exact neighborhoods seeing intense reinvestment. In the first quarter of 2024, Richmond eviction filings increased by 11% quarter-over-quarter to 3,592 filings, reaching 95% of the pre-COVID average [5]. Critically, filings have surpassed pre-pandemic levels in ZIP codes 23219, 23220, 23224, 23225, and 23230 [5]. While default judgment rates have improved (dropping to 34% in Richmond, down from pre-pandemic levels) [5], the sheer volume of filings creates immense instability. Statewide data shows that only 16-17% of filings result in executed writs of eviction [11], meaning the filing process itself is frequently used as a punitive collection tool.

## Policy and Investment Landscape
Big funding is queued for 2026, but 2024 delivery lagged. The upcoming zoning rewrite is the primary leverage point to align physical growth with equity goals.

### Affordable Housing Trust Fund and ARPA Pipeline
The City is making substantial investments to counter affordability pressures. Using American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) funding, the City has invested in projects expected to add roughly 2,135 affordable housing units to the supply, mostly coming online in 2026 [6]. Additionally, the City recently awarded $9 million from the Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF) [7]. This includes $7 million distributed across eight projects to create nearly 600 affordable units, and $2.2 million to bolster the Healthy Homes Rehabilitation program [7]. In PY24, the Healthy Homes program successfully provided emergency rehabilitation to 81 qualifying households [6]. However, delivery timing is a major risk: in the PY24 CAPER period, the City reported zero new renter-occupied housing units produced through its federal CDBG/HOME programs [6]. 

### Code Refresh Status and Levers
Richmond is currently undergoing a major update to its zoning rules, known as "Code Refresh," which began in February 2024 and released its second draft in November 2025 [8]. Through the Richmond 300 master plan, the City has a publicly stated commitment to develop 1,000 new affordable housing units by 2026 and advance zoning policies that support compact, walkable, mixed-use development [2]. The final adoption of Code Refresh will dictate where density concentrates for decades.

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

*Takeaway: The City has successfully capitalized a massive pipeline of future units, but the lack of immediate deliveries requires front-loading predevelopment grants and strictly enforcing milestones to ensure 2026 targets are met.*

## Data Infrastructure
Public data exists to track these changes, but it is highly fragmented, slowing proactive management.

### Sources and Access
Richmond provides several local data sources: the EnerGov Online Permit Portal handles building, electrical, and zoning permits [12] [13]; the Open Data Portal provides broader civic datasets [14]; and Legistar tracks Planning Commission cases. For housing metrics, the HUD CHAS API provides comprehensive cost-burden data (updated December 2025 for 2018-2022 estimates) [15], while the RVA Eviction Lab tracks hyper-local eviction filings by ZIP code [16] [5].

### Gaps and Fixes
The primary gap is integration. There is no turnkey, neighborhood-level pipeline dataset that allows policymakers to view permits, rezonings, rent changes, and evictions in a single dashboard. The EnerGov portal requires manual searches by address or project [13] [17], and CHAS data lags by several years [15]. To fix this, the City should build an integrated "Neighborhood Change Dashboard" that scrapes EnerGov and Legistar, joins it with Eviction Lab ZIP data, and flags hot spots monthly to trigger policy responses.

| Data Source | Level of Detail | Key Metrics Tracked | Accessibility | Integration Notes |

*Takeaway: Richmond has the raw data required to track displacement, but it must be synthesized into a single geographic information system (GIS) to be useful for real-time policy decisions.*

## Priority Neighborhoods for the Thriving Neighborhoods Pillar
Based on the intersection of development pressure, rising rents, and eviction risk, the following five neighborhoods are the most critical for immediate intervention:

1. **Scott’s Addition (ZIP 23230)**: The epicenter of the city's multifamily boom. With massive pipelines and cooling rent growth, the risk here is an overconcentration of luxury product that fails to serve workforce households.
2. **Manchester (ZIP 23224)**: Experiencing rapid infill along the riverfront and commercial corridors, but simultaneously suffering from eviction filings that are 104% of pre-pandemic levels [5].
3. **Jackson/Monroe Ward (ZIP 23219/23220)**: Rich in cultural assets and facing intense infill pressure from VCU and downtown employers. Eviction filings in 23219 are at 166% of pre-pandemic levels [5].
4. **Shockoe Bottom (ZIP 23219/23223)**: High TOD momentum driven by Main Street Station and Pulse BRT. The City has active site control (e.g., 212 N. 18th Street) to dictate equitable terms [2].
5. **Church Hill (ZIP 23223)**: Facing adjacency pressure from Shockoe Bottom's rapid reinvestment, requiring proactive preservation of existing affordable stock.

## Actions and Near-Term Decisions
To lock in affordability and anti-displacement where approvals and funds are moving now, the City should adopt a phased roadmap:

**0–6 Months:**
* **Condition AHTF Awards:** Tie the recently awarded $7 million in AHTF contracts [7] to ZIP-specific anti-eviction outcomes and strict delivery milestones.
* **Set RFP Floors:** Establish mandatory affordability floors (e.g., 20% of units at or below 60% AMI) for all City-owned parcels, starting with the 212 N. 18th Street site in Shockoe Bottom [2].
* **Launch Right-to-Counsel:** Direct eviction diversion funds to launch right-to-counsel pilots specifically in ZIPs 23219, 23220, and 23224, where filings have vastly exceeded 2019 baselines [5].
* **Shape Code Refresh:** Advocate for the inclusion of Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) density bonuses and parking reform in the final Code Refresh adoption [8].

**6–18 Months:**
* **Package Site Assembly:** Proactively assemble sites and provide gap financing for mixed-income developments in Manchester and Shockoe Bottom.
* **Publish Dashboard:** Launch the integrated Neighborhood Change Dashboard to track permits, rents, and evictions transparently.
* **Negotiate Infill:** Use the cooling rent growth environment [3] to negotiate stronger inclusionary terms in Scott's Addition, as developers will increasingly need public gap financing or density bonuses to make deals pencil out.

## Risks and Watchouts
* **Delivery Slippage:** The City is heavily relying on 2026 for the delivery of 2,135 ARPA-funded units and 1,000 master plan units [6] [2]. Construction delays or financing gaps could leave vulnerable residents without options as evictions rise.
* **Luxury Overbuild:** With 6,627 units underway [3], an oversupply of luxury units in Scott's Addition could lead to high vacancy in premium tiers while the 53% of renters who are cost-burdened [10] see no relief.
* **Code Refresh Dilution:** Political headwinds could water down the density and mixed-use provisions in the Code Refresh [8], pushing development back into sprawling, auto-dependent patterns.
* **Mitigations:** Implement milestone-based funding for all AHTF/ARPA awards, diversify the product mix via zoning, and track absorption rates monthly.

## Unknowns and Research Next Steps
Answering the following data gaps will sharpen the City's targeting:
* **Unknowns:** Exact building permit counts aggregated by neighborhood; the specific unit mix and AMI targets of the 6,627 units currently in the pipeline; the exact geographic overlap between Legistar rezoning cases and Eviction Lab hotspots; and neighborhood-level (rather than city-level) rent series.
* **Next Steps:** Extract the newly released 2018-2022 CHAS data via API to get updated tract-level cost burdens [15]; scrape the EnerGov portal to compile a definitive neighborhood pipeline [13]; and procure granular neighborhood rent feeds to replace macro-level Apartment List data.
