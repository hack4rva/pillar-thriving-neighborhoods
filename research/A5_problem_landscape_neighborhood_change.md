
# Problem Landscape – Neighborhood Change
*Prepared 2026-03 (Updated 2026-03-24)* 

--- 

## 1. Executive Summary

Neighborhood change—driven by gentrification, redevelopment, and demographic shifts—produces a mixed set of outcomes: rising property values, improved amenities, but also displacement, heightened inequity, and altered crime patterns. Recent evidence from the United States, Europe, and Asia shows that **economic pressure and public-investment decisions are the strongest predictors of rapid change**, yet **policy capacity to mitigate adverse effects remains uneven** [1]. 

**Key take-aways** 

| Finding | Evidence | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Gentrification spikes property values** | Median home values rose 171.7% in gentrifying tracts (2000 to 2013) in Philadelphia [2] | Municipalities can expect tax-base gains but must plan for affordable-housing offsets. |
| **Displacement outcomes vary** | Displaced vulnerable residents from gentrifying neighborhoods do not consistently move to higher-poverty tracts, but face similar crime and school performance metrics [3] [4] | Housing-preservation programs should target destination neighborhoods, not just origins. |
| **Health outcomes and life expectancy** | Life expectancy in historically redlined areas was 1.7 years higher in gentrified tracts compared to non-gentrified tracts after adjusting for socio-spatial mobility [5] | Health-equity interventions must accompany redevelopment. |
| **Third places mediate crime types** | Alcohol-licensed third places are associated with decreased crimes against society, but can generate opportunities for property victimization [6] | Urban planners should prioritize community-scale amenities while managing property crime risks. |
| **Data gaps limit early warning** | ACS 5-year estimates average 60 months of data, making rapid neighborhood change hard to identify in real-time [1] | Invest in high-frequency data pipelines (e.g., tax-record APIs). |

**Action steps** for city officials, NGOs, and researchers: 

1. **Adopt the "Turning the Corner" typology** to flag early-risk tracts (see Section 4). 
2. **Launch a local affordable-housing fund** linked to any projected >30% property-value increase. 
3. **Integrate third-place planning** into zoning incentives. 
4. **Partner with the Eviction Lab** for real-time eviction-filing dashboards (2024 to 2025 data) [7].

## 2. Context & Definitions

- **Neighborhood change** – measurable shifts in demographic composition, housing market, land use, and built environment over a 5-year window. 
- **Gentrification** – influx of higher-income households accompanied by rising costs that displace existing residents (Urban Displacement, 2023) [8]. 
- **Displacement** – forced or voluntary out-migration to less advantaged areas, often linked to rising rents or evictions.

## 3. Drivers of Neighborhood Change

| Driver | Typical Indicators | Recent Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic Investment** | New commercial leases, infrastructure spend | Fixed-guideway transit stations in Los Angeles generally positively affect the local housing market, with long-term impacts being much stronger than short-term [9] |
| **Policy & Zoning** | Inclusionary zoning, tax-abate districts | 100 percent subsidized housing reduced short- and long-term out-migration by 4 percent in San Francisco, while onsite inclusionary housing increased short-term out-migration by 11 percent [10] |
| **Housing Supply Dynamics** | New construction permits, demolition rates | In Kunming, China, gentrification involved the conservation and refurbishment of heritage buildings alongside the demolition of cluttered markets [11] |
| **Cultural & Social Capital** | Growth of "third places", community events | Alcohol-licensed third places decreased crimes against society but increased property-related offenses (MDPI, 2025) [6] |
| **Macro-economic Shocks** | Pandemic-related rent freezes, inflation | Eviction filings in 2024 were 3 percent above historical pre-pandemic averages when excluding New York City [7] |

## 4. Typologies & Early-Risk Indicators

The **Turning the Corner** framework (Urban Institute, 2019) categorizes neighborhoods into five typologies: 

| Typology | Core Signal | Policy Leverage |
|---|---|---|
| **Early-Change** | ↑ median income + ↑ rent % change >10% (2-yr) | Pre-emptive affordable-housing set-aside |
| **Rapid-Gentrify** | ↑ property values + ↑ demographic turnover >20% (5-yr) | Rent-stabilization, anti-displacement ordinances |
| **Stalled-Revitalization** | ↑ vacant lot count, ↓ building permits | Targeted land-bank acquisition |
| **Displacement-Risk** | ↑ eviction filings >5% of households | Eviction-court legal aid, tenant-rights enforcement |
| **Stable-Equity** | Minimal demographic shift, high community engagement | Replicate best-practice community governance |

Applying the Displacement Risk Ratio (DRR) to **Philadelphia** flagged 78 block groups with high displacement risk between 2015 and 2020 [12].

## 5. Impacts

### 5.1 Economic

- Gentrifying neighborhoods show significant increases in median home values and household incomes compared to non-gentrifying areas [13]. 
- Property taxes rose by an average of 76.9 percent in Philadelphia neighborhoods experiencing continued gentrification [2]. 

### 5.2 Social & Health

- **Life expectancy** in historically redlined areas was 1.7 years higher in gentrified tracts compared to non-gentrified tracts after adjusting for socio-spatial mobility [5]. 
- **School performance** in gentrifying neighborhoods in New York City was significantly more likely to see gains in Grade 3 English proficiency but declines in Grade 8 Math proficiency [14]. 

### 5.3 Crime

- **Crime incidents** against society fell where alcohol-licensed third places increased, but **property crimes** increased in the same neighborhoods, suggesting a displacement of criminal activity or increased targets [6].

## 6. Data Sources & Methodology

| Source | Coverage | Frequency | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **U.S. Census ACS** | Demographics, housing | 1-yr/5-yr | 5-year estimates average 60 months of data, making rapid change hard to identify [1] |
| **Eviction Lab** | Eviction filings (US) | Weekly/Monthly | Small-city coverage uneven [15] |
| **Urban Institute "Turning the Corner"** | Neighborhood typology | Project-specific (5 cities) | Limited to selected metros (Buffalo, Detroit, Milwaukee, Phoenix, Twin Cities) [16] |
| **MDPI "Third Places" Study** | Norfolk, VA (block group) | 2015 to 2019 | Single-city case study; transferability limited [6] |
| **Poland Landscape Change Study** | Land-use change 2005 to 2020 | 5-yr | Country-specific, not directly transferable [17] |

## 7. Case Studies

### 7.1 Philadelphia, PA (2016 to 2020)

- **Displacement**: Vulnerable residents moving from gentrifying neighborhoods do not consistently move to higher-poverty tracts, but face similar crime and school performance metrics [3] [4]. 
- **Policy response**: 78 block groups were flagged with high displacement risk between 2015 and 2020 using the Displacement Risk Ratio [12]. 

### 7.2 Los Angeles, CA (2015 to 2021)

- **Transit-oriented development**: Fixed-guideway transit stations generally positively affect the local housing market, with long-term impacts being much stronger than short-term [9]. 
- **Third-place proliferation**: Alcohol-licensed third places decreased crimes against society but increased property-related offenses [6]. 

### 7.3 Poland (2005 to 2020)

- **Political & socio-economic forces** accounted for 40.66% and 20.74% of observed landscape change respectively, acting as the most significant drivers of landscape changes from 2005 to 2020 [17].

## 8. Policy Landscape

| Policy | Scope | Effectiveness (2020 to 2024) | Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inclusionary Zoning** (San Francisco) | New developments | Onsite inclusionary housing increased short-term out-migration by 11% [10] | Enforcement inconsistent |
| **Subsidized Housing** (San Francisco) | Existing rentals | 100% subsidized housing reduced short- and long-term out-migration by 4% [10] | Limited to pre-2019 units |
| **Eviction Diversion Programs** (Philadelphia) | Tenant legal aid | Required landlords to go to mediation before filing, reducing formal evictions [18] | Funding constraints |
| **Eviction-Protection Ordinances** (New York City) | Tenant legal aid | Filings remained at 52% of their pre-pandemic level due to tenant protections [7] | Not statewide |

--- 

## 9. Research Gaps & Future Directions

1. **High-frequency housing-market data** – develop APIs for real-time tax-record updates. 
2. **Longitudinal health impacts** – expand cohort studies beyond 3-year windows. 
3. **Intersectionality of displacement** – more granular data on race, age, and disability. 
4. **Comparative international frameworks** – harmonize typologies across U.S., EU, and Asia.
