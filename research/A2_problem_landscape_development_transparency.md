# From "Technically Public" to "Truly Findable": Fixing How Richmond Residents Discover Development Proposals

## Executive Summary

Richmond’s development transparency problem is not rooted in secrecy, but in severe digital fragmentation. While the City meets its legal obligations for public notice, the information is scattered across multiple platforms, creating a massive discoverability gap for everyday residents. 

**Top Strategic Insights:**
* **Fragmented systems hide the story:** Planning information is split across four different sites (CivicPlus for agendas, DocumentCenter for reports, Granicus for video, and IMS for permitting) [1]. Residents must chain-hop to piece together a single proposal.
* **Narrow mailed notice radius misses real stakeholders:** Official notices confirm a minimum 300-foot mailing radius [2] [3]. *Inference:* On typical blocks, most neighbors and renters beyond a one-block radius never hear about a project until it is too late.
* **Conflicting official schedules create uncertainty:** The City's own websites offer conflicting information, with one page stating the Planning Commission meets the first Thursday of each month [1], and another stating it meets the first and third Thursdays [4]. 
* **The "Legistar" confusion siphons residents to the wrong place:** Richmond, CA does not expose a unified Legistar portal in search. Residents looking for "Richmond Legistar" are often misdirected to Contra Costa County [5] or Richmond, Virginia [6].
* **Access friction to videos limits catch-up:** Archived meeting videos on Granicus still reference requirements for Windows Media Player [7], creating accessibility barriers for mobile and modern web users.
* **Comment windows close early:** Written comments must be received by 3:00 PM on the day of the hearing with strict subject line formats [3] [8], shutting out residents who discover a case at the last minute.
* **Legal stakes are high for missed meetings:** Notices explicitly warn that under California Government Code Section 65009, court challenges may be limited to issues raised at or before the public meeting [8].
* **Plain-language and address-first discovery are missing:** The City's IMS portal requires searching by project number, APN, or exact address [1], which fails to answer the basic resident question: "What is planned near me?"
* **Benchmarks show what "discoverable" looks like:** Nearby Contra Costa County [5] and namesake Richmond, VA [6] utilize consolidated portals that drastically lower discovery friction.
* **Statutory specifics require verification:** While notices cite the Richmond Municipal Code for the 300-foot radius [9], the exact code text (likely within Article 15.04.803) requires direct verification to understand all legal posting modalities [10].

## Problem Landscape — Key Friction is Not Secrecy but Fragmentation and Misdirection

Information regarding Richmond development proposals is technically public, but it is highly scattered. Residents need a single, address-first path to see what is proposed, when it will be discussed, and what actions they can take. Currently, the City's digital infrastructure forces users to navigate a maze of disconnected systems.

The fragmentation spans four distinct platforms:
1. **Agendas:** Hosted on CivicPlus archive pages [1].
2. **Staff Reports:** Stored separately in a DocumentCenter index [1].
3. **Video/Audio:** Hosted on Granicus [1] [7] and broadcast via KCRT [11].
4. **Project Data:** Housed in the Integrated Management System (IMS) [1].

Compounding this fragmentation are conflicting official schedules. The main Planning Commission page states that meetings occur at 6:30 P.M. on the first Thursday of each month [1]. However, the City Clerk's Boards and Commissions page states that meetings are held every first and third Thursday at 6:30 P.M. [4]. *Inference:* This inconsistency makes it highly likely that residents will miss hearings by trusting the wrong official page.

The stakes for missing these early meetings are severe. Official public hearing notices warn residents that pursuant to Section 65009 of the California Government Code, any future court challenges to a project may be limited strictly to the issues raised at the public meeting or in written correspondence delivered prior to the meeting [8]. The cost of non-discovery is the total loss of legal recourse.

## Current Notification Workflow in Richmond, CA — What’s Required and What Residents Actually Experience

Richmond meets its legal minimums for public notification, but practical discovery for non-experts remains exceptionally weak. 

### 300-Foot Mail Radius Excludes Renters and Broader Neighborhoods
Multiple official City of Richmond notices confirm the current mailing standard: "all property owners within a minimum 300-foot radius of the project site are required to be notified under the Richmond Municipal Code" [2] [3] [9] [8] [12]. 

*Inference:* Because the mandate specifically targets "property owners" within a tight 300-foot geographic circle, renters living adjacent to proposed developments, as well as homeowners located just outside the immediate block, are systematically excluded from proactive notification. 

### Where and How Agendas, Reports, and Videos are Posted Today
When a resident tries to find information, they are directed to the Planning Commission website (https://www.ci.richmond.ca.us/2864/Planning-Commission). This page acts as a loose hub, providing separate links to "Hearing Agendas," "Staff Reports," and "Hearing Streaming Video - Archived" [1]. 

For video access, residents are directed to KCRT for live broadcasts [11] or to a Granicus portal for archives. The Granicus portal introduces significant friction, explicitly stating that "Windows Media Player is required to view the videos" [7]. While the City notes that viewers can also watch replays on YouTube due to occasional delays [13], the primary linked archive relies on outdated technology.

### Comment Logistics and Deadlines Reduce Late-Discovery Participation
If a resident discovers a notice late, participating is logistically difficult. Written comments sent via email or U.S. mail must be received by the Planning Division prior to 3:00 PM on the date of the meeting [3] [8]. Furthermore, the City requires strict subject line formatting, such as "Agenda Item #[insert agenda item number]" [8]. *Inference:* A resident who finds out about a controversial project at 4:00 PM on a Thursday is entirely shut out of the written administrative record.

## Where Residents Encounter "Legistar" Today — And Why That’s a Trap for Richmond CA

Searching for "Richmond Legistar" or "Richmond Planning Commission Legistar" creates a massive misdirection trap that wastes resident time and causes missed deadlines.

### Misdirection to Virginia and Contra Costa County
Because the City of Richmond, CA does not have a highly visible, unified Legistar portal for its Planning Commission, search engines fill the void with incorrect jurisdictions. Residents frequently land on:
* **Contra Costa County Legistar:** (https://contra-costa.legistar.com/) which hosts county-level Planning Commission meetings, not city-level ones [5].
* **Richmond, VA Legistar:** (https://richmondva.legistar.com/) which features a robust, fully functional portal for the Virginia capital, complete with its own "Planning Commission" department page [6] [14].

### The City’s Current Mixed-Platform Pattern
The City of Richmond, CA does use modern meeting software, but inconsistently. City Council meetings from 2022 onward are hosted on eScribe, while 2006-Present archives remain on Granicus [15]. However, the Planning Commission does not benefit from a unified eScribe or Legistar calendar, relying instead on the fragmented CivicPlus/Granicus/IMS setup [1].

## The Discoverability Gap — From "Technically Public" to "Actually Findable"

The gap between a document existing on a server and a resident actually understanding a proposal stems from specialized inputs, dispersed artifacts, and early cutoff times.

### User Journey Teardown: "A Neighbor Hears Construction Rumors"
Imagine a resident hears a rumor about a new townhome development at 5620 Central Avenue (the Cascade Townhome project) [9]. 
1. They visit the Planning Commission page and are told to use the IMS permitting system [1].
2. IMS requires them to search by project number, parcel number (APN), or exact address [1]. If they don't know the exact address or APN, the search fails.
3. If they find the project, they must then cross-reference the CivicPlus archive to find the agenda, and the DocumentCenter to find the staff report [1].
4. By the time they piece the narrative together, the 3:00 PM written comment deadline has likely passed [8].

### Failure Modes and Equity Impacts
This system disproportionately impacts vulnerable populations. Renters do not receive the 300-foot mailers [2]. Non-English speakers face highly technical, jargon-heavy staff reports. Mobile-only users are forced to download heavy PDF packets and navigate a video portal optimized for desktop Windows Media Player [7].

## Benchmarks That Raise Discoverability — What Nearby Jurisdictions Already Do

A single, searchable calendar with standardized case pages lowers discovery friction. Comparing Richmond, CA to neighboring and namesake jurisdictions reveals a stark contrast in usability.

### Meetings and Proposal Discoverability Features

| Jurisdiction / Portal | Single Calendar Across Bodies | Case/Agenda Packet Consistency | Search by Address | Email/RSS Alerts |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **City of Richmond, CA** (Current) | No (Split across CivicPlus, Granicus, IMS) | Mixed (Agendas and reports stored separately) | IMS only (Requires exact address/APN) | Limited (Manual mailers) |
| **Contra Costa County** (Legistar) | Yes (Unified Legistar index) | Yes (Agenda, minutes, video in one view) | No direct address search | Yes (Legistar alerts) |
| **City of Richmond, VA** (Legistar) | Yes (Unified Legistar index) | Yes (Agenda, minutes, video in one view) | No direct address search | Yes (Legistar alerts) |

**Takeaways:** Both Contra Costa County [5] and Richmond, VA [6] utilize unified portals that aggregate agendas, minutes, and videos into a single view. Richmond, CA must borrow this consolidated calendar approach—either by extending its existing City Council eScribe contract to the Planning Commission or adopting a Legistar-like index.

## Primary Friction Points and Which Are Software-Addressable First

Six fast, software-addressable moves can materially cut missed proposals within one quarter.

### Top 6 Priorities (In Order of Impact)
1. **Canonical Meetings Hub + ICS Feeds:** Designate a single, canonical schedule page for the Planning Commission to resolve the 1st vs. 3rd Thursday conflict [1] [4]. Embed an always-current ICS calendar feed.
2. **SEO and Redirects:** Implement SEO page titles (e.g., "City of Richmond, California — Planning Commission Agendas") and a "Not in Richmond, VA?" interstitial to capture users lost to the Legistar search trap [6].
3. **Address-First "What's Near Me?" Map:** Build a public-facing map sourced from IMS and agenda metadata that allows residents to drop a pin and see all active cases within a custom radius, bypassing the need for APNs [1].
4. **Unified Project Pages:** Stand up a single page per active case that auto-aggregates the agenda link, staff report, next hearing date, and the 3:00 PM comment deadline [3].
5. **Geofenced Alerts & Renter Sign-ups:** Keep the 300-foot mailer for legal compliance [2], but build an opt-in email/SMS alert system geofenced to 1,000+ feet, allowing renters to subscribe by building address.
6. **Video Accessibility Upgrades:** Mandate that all Planning Commission videos are cross-posted to YouTube with timestamps and auto-generated transcripts, deprecating the reliance on Granicus/Windows Media Player [7] [13].

### Policy Tweaks to Consider
The City should standardize subject-line requirements for public comments to reduce rejection rates [8], translate standard notices into Spanish, and explicitly publish the exact Municipal Code section governing the 300-foot radius on all mailers to build trust.

## Implementation Plan and KPIs — Prove We Moved the Needle

To ensure these changes actually improve discoverability, the City must measure discovery, not just pageviews.

* **30 Days:** Launch the canonical meetings hub, fix SEO routing, and standardize YouTube video uploads.
* **60-90 Days:** Launch the address-first map integration pulling from IMS.
* **90-120 Days:** Roll out unified project pages and geofenced SMS/email alerts.

**Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):**
* Time-to-find: Reduce the time it takes a user to go from an address search to a project page to under 30 seconds.
* Alert Subscriptions: Achieve a 50% increase in digital neighborhood alert subscribers.
* Plain Language: Ensure 75% of active cases feature a plain-language summary.
* Complaint Reduction: Baseline and reduce the number of "I was not notified" public comments in meeting minutes.

## Risks and Mitigations — Legal, Data, and Capacity

Most risks associated with this digital transformation are mitigable with basic content governance.

* **Data Accuracy:** Syncing IMS data with agenda links could result in broken links. *Mitigation:* Implement nightly sync scripts and assign a dedicated content owner within the Planning Division.
* **Legal Clarity on Notice Extensions:** Expanding digital notices beyond the 300-foot statutory minimum [2] could create confusion about legal standing. *Mitigation:* Have the City Attorney review all non-statutory alerts to ensure they include disclaimers that they do not alter Gov Code 65009 requirements [8].
