# BlueSky Problem Statements — Thriving Neighborhoods

These broader framings are useful for inspiration. Teams should narrow them before building.

## Reimagining Attainable Homeownership — 19/27 (Needs work)
How might we use technology to help workforce households in Richmond — teachers, nurses, firefighters, City employees earning roughly 80–120% of Area Median Income — understand their realistic homeownership options and navigate available programs so that more families can access stable, affordable housing?

Notes: Households in the 80–120% AMI range are increasingly priced out of starter homes. Rising prices and limited housing product types create a gap between "affordable" housing programs (which often serve lower-income households) and households that earn too much to qualify but too little to compete. The rubric score of 19/27 reflects unclear constraints, limited verified data, and risk of overclaiming (affordability calculations without authoritative data are misleading). Teams must avoid presenting calculators as authoritative eligibility guidance.

## Making Neighborhood Change Easier to Understand — 22/27 (Strong) ★ Recommended
How might we use technology to help Richmond residents understand how their neighborhoods are changing — so that they can engage more meaningfully in planning, investment, and development decisions that affect where they live?

Notes: This blue sky is closest to the targeted Neighborhood Development Discovery statement and inherits much of its strength. Clear user (Richmond resident), strong data path (Legistar, GeoHub, Socrata), and a natural demo path (map or timeline of development activity). Teams should scope tightly to one neighborhood or one data layer rather than attempting a citywide dashboard. Recommended entry point into this pillar.

## Unlocking More Housing Through Smarter Regulation — 18/27 (Needs work)
How might we use technology to identify regulatory barriers to housing production in Richmond — such as restrictive zoning, lengthy permitting processes, or underutilized land — so that more housing can be built faster and in the right places?

Notes: This framing scores 18/27 because it depends heavily on policy change, requires sophisticated data analysis across multiple systems, and has no clear weekend-feasible demo path. Zoning reform and permitting acceleration are inherently political and institutional, not software problems. Teams interested in housing supply should consider narrowing to a visualization or explainer tool (e.g., showing where by-right housing is permitted) rather than a regulatory analysis platform. Regulatory recommendations require City expertise and legal review that is outside hackathon scope.
